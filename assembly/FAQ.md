# Assembly FAQ

Common questions about taking part in the SCI for OpenTelemetry Assembly. For the full picture, see the Participant Handout.

## How the Assembly works

- **How many rounds will there be?** It depends on the section. Where the group already broadly agrees, a section can move quickly; where opinion is divided, it takes longer. Each phase has a cap, so no section can run on indefinitely.
- **Who decides when we move on?** The facilitator surfaces where things stand and recommends; Sarah Hsu, as project lead, decides the transition. The facilitator never takes a position on the substance.
- **What if I miss a round?** For Discover, the gating rule means you rejoin at the next section. For Decide, silence is recorded as consent. If you know you will be away, a quick note is appreciated but not required.
- **Can I change my mind mid-section?** Yes, within a phase. Minds visibly changing is the strongest sign the process is working. Once a section closes, it does not reopen.
- **What if I am not an expert in a particular section?** Every round carries the core questions plus an optional technical deep-dive. Answer at your level. The Research Note's two tracks are there to level the ground.
- **Deadlines say "Anywhere on Earth". What does that mean?** As long as the deadline date hasn't ended in the last time zone on the planet, you are on time. Practically: if it is still that date anywhere in the world, your reply counts. No time zone is ever penalised.
- **How much time will this take?** Expect 30 to 45 minutes in an active week, for roughly 12 to 16 weeks. Some weeks are quieter. The one habit that matters is replying within each round's window.

## AI, synthesis and accuracy

- **How is AI used in this Assembly, and does it decide anything?** No. The AI reads everyone's responses and drafts summaries and candidate positions to react to. It never makes a decision. Every rating, every vote and every objection is made by you and the other participants, and the facilitator reviews what the AI produces before it reaches you. It is a lens that helps the group see its own thinking, not a judge.
- **What if the synthesis gets my point wrong?** You will see it, and you can correct it. Every synthesis is reviewed before it goes out, each one opens by showing what changed because of the group's input, and you get to respond every round. If a candidate has missed something you care about, rate it low and say why. A position that leaves someone behind does not go forward.
- **Can I check that my response was actually counted?** Yes. The process keeps a verifiable record, and you can ask for confirmation that your exact response was included in its round. This is how we make the process auditable without ever attaching your name to what you said.
- **Doesn't running AI carry its own carbon cost?** It is not free, but it is far smaller than what it replaces. Deliberating by email avoids the travel and repeated video calls a group this size would otherwise need, which is where the real emissions sit. For a foundation focused on software's footprint, running the process lightly is part of the point.

## Participation experience

- **Will this feel impersonal, with everything happening over email?** The written, asynchronous format is a deliberate choice, not a cost saving. It gives everyone equal space to make their case, so the outcome turns on the best argument rather than on who is most senior, most fluent, or quickest to reply. We also know a group is more than its arguments, which is why the optional drop-in calls exist purely for connection. The thinking happens in writing; the human side happens there.
- **Do I need to install anything?** No. Everything works by replying to email.
- **Does my writing style matter? English is not my first language.** Not at all. We care about your reasoning, not your prose. Write in whatever style suits you, use bullet points, or dictate your answer with speech-to-text. Nothing is graded on polish.
- **How can something this complex be settled in 200 words per section?** Because the blueprint agrees direction, not specification text. The formal conventions are drafted afterwards from what the group settles. Short consensus text is deliberate: it forces the group to decide what matters most, which is the actual deliberation. Unlimited space produces documents that include everyone's opinion and represent no one's agreement.

## Privacy, visibility and role

- **Can other participants see what I write?** In Discover, no; only an anonymised synthesis is shared back. In Deliberate, yes; that phase is a visible conversation so you can respond to each other. Nothing is ever published outside the Assembly with your name against it.
- **Am I taking part as myself or on behalf of my employer?** As yourself. You were selected for your expertise, not as an organisational representative, and nothing you write is published with your name or your employer's. Participants from non-member organisations sign an Individual Contributor Agreement during onboarding, which covers the intellectual property side and means the final blueprint can be used and submitted cleanly. If your employer has questions about that agreement, ask us; we have seen most versions of this conversation before.
- **I maintain a tool in this space. Is that a conflict of interest?** No, it is part of why you are here. Practical experience of what can actually be measured is exactly what the blueprint needs. The process stays neutral by design: proposals are always framed around what any backend must be able to compute, never around what any particular tool emits, and no tool or vendor is given special standing.

## Consensus, voting and disagreement

- **What do Endorse, Consent and Object actually mean?** Endorse means you actively support the proposal and put your name behind it. Consent means you can live with it; not your ideal, but not something you would block. Object means you cannot accept it as written, and an objection always comes with an explanation of what would need to change. Consent is not a lesser vote; most healthy consensus is built on it.
- **I disagree with a proposal, but I don't want to be difficult. Should I just consent?** No. Objecting is not confrontational here; it is the mechanism doing its job, and it is the only moment your disagreement can change the text, because settled sections do not reopen. An objection starts a conversation: you say what would need to change, the group works on it, and a revised proposal comes back. A consent you don't mean is the one move that genuinely harms the process.
- **One of the candidates seems designed to be disagreed with. Why?** It is. Each Deliberate round presents three candidates, and one of them deliberately amplifies under-represented viewpoints and challenges where the group seems to be heading. Explaining why you reject something reveals what you actually care about, often more than explaining what you like. It is there to sharpen the group's thinking, not to trick anyone.
- **What if the group later realises a settled section was a mistake?** Two safety nets exist. Section 5 ends with a worked example that tests whether everything settled actually composes into a computable SCI; if a gap appears, that section adds the missing piece. And after the Assembly, everyone is invited to review the drafted conventions against the blueprint before submission. What forward-only prevents is relitigating decisions; it does not prevent fixing genuine breaks.

## Outputs and OpenTelemetry process

- **How does this relate to OpenTelemetry's own process?** The Assembly does the hard alignment work up front. We produce the agreed design, and the formal conventions are then written from it and carried to the OpenTelemetry Semantic Conventions SIG, the group that governs them, as the community's proposed design. Settling the difficult trade-offs here, among the right experts, means the formal process is not where those arguments have to happen.
- **What actually gets delivered at the end?** A report capturing each section's settled consensus, the reasoning, the votes, and a worked example of the full SCI computation. This blueprint is what the draft conventions are built from.
- **What happens after the blueprint is agreed?** The blueprint is rendered into draft semantic conventions after the Assembly closes. You will be invited to review that rendering to confirm it faithfully reflects what the group agreed, before it is submitted to the OpenTelemetry Semantic Conventions SIG. Your involvement does not end at the blueprint.
